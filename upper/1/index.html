Olympiad Math Practice Worksheet 1

Grade 5-6 | Number Theory

Questions: Divisibility & Remainders

1. If the 6-digit number $472AB8$ is divisible by 88, find the value of $A - B$.

2. Find the smallest four-digit number that leaves a remainder of 3 when divided by 5, a remainder of 5 when divided by 7, and a remainder of 7 when divided by 9.

3. What is the units digit of the sum $13^{2023} + 17^{2024} + 19^{2025}$?

4. The 5-digit number $7A52A$ is divisible by 9. What is the value of A?

5. What is the remainder when $2^{100}$ is divided by 7?

6. The five-digit number $1234x$ is divisible by 8. What is the value of $x$?

7. Find the smallest positive integer that leaves a remainder of 1 when divided by 2, a remainder of 2 when divided by 3, and a remainder of 3 when divided by 4.

8. If the 8-digit number $123456AB$ is divisible by both 8 and 9, what are all possible values for $A+B$?

9. What is the units digit of $2022^{2023} \times 2023^{2024}$?

10. A number $N$ leaves a remainder of 9 when divided by 10, a remainder of 8 when divided by 9, ..., and a remainder of 1 when divided by 2. Find the smallest such positive integer $N$.

11. The 5-digit number $7A6B2$ is divisible by 72. Find the sum of all possible values of $A+B$.

12. What is the remainder when $(1! + 2! + 3! + \dots + 50!)$ is divided by 15?

13. How many simplified proper fractions are there with a denominator of 120?

14. What is the units digit of $1! + 3! + 5! + \dots + 99!$?

15. A 4-digit number leaves a remainder of 2 when divided by 5, 7, and 11. What is the smallest such 4-digit number?

16. The 5-digit number $5A83B$ is divisible by 99. Find the value of $B-A$.

17. Find the remainder when the number formed by writing the digit 7 for 2023 times (i.e., 777...7) is divided by 9.

18. What is the units digit of $7^{7^7}$?

19. The number $8765A3B$ is divisible by 72. Find the value of $2A+B$.

20. A positive integer $n$ leaves a remainder of 7 when divided by 9. What is the remainder when $4n + 3$ is divided by 9?

1. Solution: (Corrected)

Concept: For a number to be divisible by 88, it must be divisible by its co-prime factors, 8 and 11.
Rule for 8: A number is divisible by 8 if its last three digits form a number divisible by 8.
Rule for 11: A number is divisible by 11 if the alternating sum of its digits is a multiple of 11.

Step 1 (Divisibility by 8): The number is `472AB8`. The last three digits are `AB8`. A number `100A + 10B + 8` must be divisible by 8. Since `100A` is `96A + 4A` and `10B` is `8B + 2B`, and `96A`, `8B`, `8` are all divisible by 8, the condition simplifies to `4A + 2B` must be divisible by 8. Dividing by 2, we find that `2A + B` must be a multiple of 4.

Step 2 (Divisibility by 11): We calculate the alternating sum of the digits of `472AB8`. Start from the right: $(8) - (B) + (A) - (2) + (7) - (4) = A - B + 9$. This sum must be a multiple of 11.

Step 3 (Combine Rules and Find A and B): From Step 2, $A - B + 9$ could be 0, 11, -11, etc.
- If $A - B + 9 = 0 \implies B - A = 9$. The only digits that satisfy this are $B=9, A=0$. Let's test this pair with our rule from Step 1: `2A + B = 2(0) + 9 = 9`. Is 9 a multiple of 4? No. This case is invalid.
- If $A - B + 9 = 11 \implies A - B = 2$. This is a strong possibility. Let's call this Equation (i).
- If $A - B + 9 = 22 \implies A - B = 13$. Impossible for single digits. So we must have `A - B = 2`, which means `A = B + 2`.

Step 4 (Substitute and Solve): Now substitute `A = B + 2` into our rule from Step 1 (`2A + B` must be a multiple of 4).
`2(B + 2) + B = 4k` (where k is an integer)
`2B + 4 + B = 4k`
`3B + 4 = 4k` Since 4 is a multiple of 4, for `3B + 4` to be a multiple of 4, `3B` must also be a multiple of 4. As 3 and 4 share no common factors, B itself must be a multiple of 4.

Step 5 (Check Possible Values for B):
- If B=0, then A = 0 + 2 = 2. Pair: (A,B)=(2,0). $A-B = 2$.
- If B=4, then A = 4 + 2 = 6. Pair: (A,B)=(6,4). $A-B = 2$.

Let N be the number.

$N \div 7$ leaves remainder 5 (which is $7-2$).
$N \div 9$ leaves remainder 7 (which is $9-2$).

Step 2: We need to find a number that is a multiple of 5, 7, and 9. The smallest such positive number is the LCM(5, 7, 9). Since 5, 7, and 9 are co-prime, their LCM is their product: $5 \times 7 \times 9 = 315$.

Step 3: So, $N+2$ must be a multiple of 315. The general form is $N+2 = 315k$ for some integer k. This means $N = 315k - 2$.

Step 4: We are looking for the smallest four-digit number N.

5. Solution:

Concept (Modular Arithmetic & Fermat's Little Theorem): We can find the remainder by looking for a pattern in the remainders of powers. A shortcut is Fermat's Little Theorem, which states that if p is a prime number, then for any integer a, $a^p \equiv a \pmod{p}$. A common corollary is if a is not divisible by p, then $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$.

Method 1: Finding a pattern
Step 1: Find the remainders of powers of 2 when divided by 7:
$2^1 \div 7$ leaves remainder 2.
$2^2 = 4 \div 7$ leaves remainder 4.
$2^3 = 8 \div 7$ leaves remainder 1.
Step 2: We found a cycle. The remainder becomes 1 at $2^3$. This means every group of $2^3$ contributes a remainder of 1.
Step 3: We need to see how many cycles of 3 are in the exponent 100. $100 \div 3 = 33$ with a remainder of 1.
Step 4: We can rewrite $2^{100}$ as $2^{3 \times 33 + 1} = (2^3)^{33} \times 2^1$.
Step 5: The remainder of $(2^3)^{33}$ is the same as $1^{33}$, which is 1. The remainder of $2^1$ is 2.
Step 6: The final remainder is $1 \times 2 = 2$.

Method 2: Using Fermat's Little Theorem
Step 1: The divisor is 7, which is a prime number. The base is 2, which is not divisible by 7.
Step 2: According to the theorem, $2^{7-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$, so $2^6 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$.
Step 3: Rewrite the exponent 100 in terms of 6. $100 \div 6 = 16$ with a remainder of 4.
Step 4: So, $2^{100} = 2^{6 \times 16 + 4} = (2^6)^{16} \times 2^4$.
Step 5: The remainder of $(2^6)^{16}$ is $1^{16} = 1$. The remainder of $2^4 = 16$ when divided by 7 is 2.
Step 6: The final remainder is $1 \times 2 = 2$.

Final Answer: 2

13. Solution:

Concept (Euler's Totient Function): A simplified proper fraction with denominator n has a numerator that is less than n and co-prime to n. The number of such numerators is given by Euler's totient function, $\phi(n)$.
Formula: If $n = p_1^{k_1} p_2^{k_2} \dots p_r^{k_r}$, then $\phi(n) = n(1 - \frac{1}{p_1})(1 - \frac{1}{p_2})\dots(1 - \frac{1}{p_r})$.

Step 1: Find the prime factorization of the denominator, 120.
$120 = 12 \times 10 = (2^2 \times 3) \times (2 \times 5) = 2^3 \times 3^1 \times 5^1$.
The distinct prime factors are 2, 3, and 5.

Step 2: Apply Euler's totient function formula.
$\phi(120) = 120 \times (1 - \frac{1}{2}) \times (1 - \frac{1}{3}) \times (1 - \frac{1}{5})$
$\phi(120) = 120 \times (\frac{1}{2}) \times (\frac{2}{3}) \times (\frac{4}{5})$

Step 3: Calculate the result.
$\phi(120) = 60 \times (\frac{2}{3}) \times (\frac{4}{5}) = 40 \times (\frac{4}{5}) = 32$.

Final Answer: 32
